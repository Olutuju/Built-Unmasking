I came up around finance, so I'll talk about money the way I actually think about it. If you're a chronically ill founder in Austin raising or managing capital, there's a variable in your model that nobody on a term sheet is pricing: **you.** Your capacity is the input everyone assumes is constant and you know isn't. Here's how I reason about it.

## Model your capacity like any material risk

Every credible financial model names its key risks. Yours has one more: the founder's own reliable output isn't a constant. That's not a weakness to hide, it's a variable to **model**, exactly like you'd model churn or a supply risk.

Practically: when you build runway, don't build it off your best-case output. Build in the reality that some weeks you'll be at 40%. A runway that only works if you never get sick isn't a runway; it's a hope. This is the same [capacity-forecasting discipline](/blog/energy-management-as-business-strategy-austin/) applied to the balance sheet.

## Disclosure is yours to control

The anxious question is always: **do I have to tell investors?** Generally, no, you're under no obligation to disclose a diagnosis, and the vast majority of founders don't. But the better frame isn't "how do I hide it." It's "how do I build a company whose resilience makes it a non-issue."

Investors are underwriting durability. So **show them durability**, structurally:

- Documented systems, so the company runs without your hands on everything.
- A real team, so you're not a single point of failure.
- Realistic plans that hit, because you built in [buffers for the bad weeks](/blog/building-work-around-flares-austin-operators/).

A founder who has designed genuine operational redundancy is **more** fundable, not less, because that's a more resilient company, full stop. You're not compensating for a liability. You're demonstrating a competence most founders lack.

> Don't ask investors to bet on you never getting sick. Build a company that's a good bet even when you do. That's a better company anyway.

## Favor models that don't require heroics

Here's a strategic bias worth holding: **capital-efficient, cash-generating businesses suit us better than burn-heavy ones.** A company that requires the founder to sprint at 100% indefinitely to justify its burn is a bad fit for a body that can't guarantee the sprint. A business that throws off cash and can scale deliberately gives you options and control.

[Paul Graham's essays](https://www.paulgraham.com/articles.html) on default-alive versus default-dead companies land differently when your personal runway is also finite, being default-alive isn't just financial safety, it's **health** safety. Austin has a strong bootstrapped and capital-efficient founder scene precisely because you don't need to move to Silicon Valley to build that way here. Lean into it.
