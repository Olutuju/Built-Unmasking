I want to say something plainly that a lot of chronically ill people feel and rarely see written down: **when you've been failed by medical providers, being sent to another medical website is not help. It's salt.**

## The trust is broken for a reason

If you live with a chronic condition, you probably have a collection of stories. The specialist who didn't read your chart. The dismissal dressed up as reassurance. The test that cost a fortune and explained nothing. The distinct experience of being treated as a difficult patient for the crime of still being sick.

So here's the thing nobody in healthcare wants to hear: **for many of us, "go to a medical site" means "go read something written by the exact institution that failed you."** That's not a trusted source. That's the source of the injury. And it's why so much well-meaning "resource" content bounces right off us, it's coming from the wrong direction.

This is not anti-medicine. Real providers help real people every day. It's just honest about a broken trust that a lot of health content pretends isn't there.

## The knowledge you actually need doesn't live in a clinic

Think about what you're actually trying to figure out as a chronically ill founder:

- How do I run a pitch meeting on a bad day?
- How do I structure my company so a flare doesn't sink it?
- How do I network in Austin without wrecking myself?
- How do I tell a client no without torching the relationship?

**None of that is medical.** No doctor is trained in it, and honestly, most have never had to solve it. That knowledge (the how-to-actually-live-and-work-around-this) lives entirely in the community of people doing it. It's peer knowledge, and it's the single most underserved thing in the whole chronic-illness experience.

> Your doctor can, at best, help with the disease. Only your peers can help with the life you're building around it.

## Peer to peer, on purpose

That's why [Built & Unmasking](/) is not, and will never be, a medical resource. No treatment advice. No "have you tried" from people who haven't lived it. Just accomplished people in Austin who are solving the same real problems and comparing notes as equals.

The research on peer support in other contexts is strong: [Stanford Social Innovation Review](https://ssir.org/) and others have documented for years how peer models create change that expert-delivered services can't. But honestly, you don't need a citation for this one. You already know the difference between talking to someone who **gets it** and someone who's read about it.

## Find your people

The medical system will do what it does. Meanwhile, the most valuable thing you can build for yourself isn't another appointment, it's a room of people who don't need the situation explained. That's what I'm making here in Austin: [a monthly gathering downtown](/events/) and a network of peers, no white coats required.

If you're tired of being handed pamphlets and want people instead, [come find your people](/events/).
