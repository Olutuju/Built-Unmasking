Every founder pays to build. Cash, hours, sleep, relationships — the tuition is real and everybody knows it going in. But if you're building a business while chronically ill, you're paying a second bill nobody warned you about. I call it the hidden tax, and the first step to not overpaying is to write it down.

## What the tax actually covers

The normal costs of entrepreneurship are visible. This tax is the surcharge on top, and it comes due in three currencies:

**Time.** Days lost to flares. Recovery days after the "good" day you overspent. The morning you needed and didn't have. [The SBA](https://www.sba.gov/) will tell you most small businesses run lean on time already; now subtract the days your body reclaims without asking.

**Energy.** The specific, exhausting labor of *masking* — showing up to the Austin pitch meeting looking like the version of you that slept eight hours, when you slept two. Sociologists have studied this as emotional labor for decades; every chronically ill professional pays it and almost nobody expenses it.

**Redundancy.** Healthy founders can be a single point of failure and mostly get away with it. You can't. So you build backup — documented processes, a second person who can run the thing, buffers in every timeline. That redundancy is expensive up front and it is not optional.

## Why naming it in numbers matters

Founders are trained to respect what they can measure. [Harvard Business Review](https://hbr.org/) has been beating the "manage your energy, not just your time" drum for years, and it lands harder when you actually run the math.

So run it. How many working days a quarter does your illness realistically take? What does an hour of masking cost you in output the next day? How much slower is your launch calendar when you build in honest buffers? Put real figures on it. A cost you refuse to name gets paid anyway — in missed deadlines, blown launches, and the quiet shame of "why can't I keep up," which is the most expensive line item of all.

> You are not bad at business. You are being taxed at a rate your competitors aren't, and pretending otherwise is how you go bankrupt on capacity.

## Designing around the tax in Austin

You can't get the tax repealed. You can stop overpaying:

- **Price it into the plan.** Build launch timelines and client commitments around your real capacity, not your best day. See [energy management as a business strategy](/blog/energy-management-as-business-strategy-austin/).
- **Buy down single points of failure early.** [Delegate before you're desperate](/blog/delegation-when-you-cant-do-it-all-austin-founder/), not during a flare.
- **Choose a model that bends.** Some Austin businesses assume your body; some don't. Pick the second kind — more on [building a business that bends](/blog/remote-hybrid-in-person-austin-business-that-bends/).

The founders I know here who've made peace with this aren't working less hard. They're just done paying the shame surtax on top of the real one. That alone gives you a quarter of your capacity back.
