The hardest part of networking with a chronic illness isn't the event. It's the uncertainty: you commit on Monday to a Thursday coffee with no idea which body will show up Thursday. So most of us either over-commit and crash, or opt out entirely and go invisible. There's a third way. Here's the playbook I actually use in Austin.

## Rule 1: Plan against your average, not your peak

Founders are optimists, it's practically a job requirement. But scheduling from your best day is how you end up canceling from your worst. Look at a real month and ask what your **median** capacity is. Build your networking calendar for that person. [HBR's work on managing energy over time](https://hbr.org/) applies double to us: the resource you're rationing isn't hours, it's usable ones.

## Rule 2: Keep scripts loaded

Half the cost of a flare is the scramble to explain it. Remove the scramble. Keep these ready:

- **Reschedule:** "Something came up health-wise. I need to move our coffee. I'd still love to connect. Next week?"
- **Leave early:** "I've got a hard stop at 11, this was great, let's continue over email."
- **Decline:** "Evenings are tough for me right now; I do my best connecting over a daytime coffee. Want to grab one?"

Notice none of them hand over a diagnosis. You owe people a reschedule, not your chart. Setting that boundary cleanly is a skill. I wrote a whole piece on [boundaries like an executive](/blog/setting-boundaries-like-an-executive-austin/).

## Rule 3: Let async carry the weight

The standing-room model pretends the only real connection happens live and loud. It doesn't. A thoughtful email, a genuinely useful intro, a comment that shows you actually read someone's work, these build relationships on your schedule, from your couch, on a bad day. [Paul Graham has written](https://www.paulgraham.com/articles.html) about how the best professional relationships are built on being genuinely useful over time, not on working a room once. That is **good** news for us.

## Rule 4: Trade up to warm introductions

Cold networking is a numbers game that burns energy you can't spare. Warm intros are the opposite, one well-placed "you two should know each other" from someone who gets it replaces ten cold coffees. This is the entire logic of a curated community: you're not hunting the whole city, you're plugged into a small trusted graph. It's why I stopped trying to attend everything in Austin and [built one room](/blog/building-a-chronic-entrepreneur-network-in-austin/) instead.

## Rule 5: Give yourself real permission to opt out

> A relationship you preserved by rescheduling is worth infinitely more than one you torched by pushing through and showing up as a wreck.

The guilt of canceling is often more damaging than the cancellation. Serious people (the ex-operators and founders you actually want) understand rescheduling. The ones who penalize you for it just told you they aren't your people. That's not a loss. That's a filter working.
