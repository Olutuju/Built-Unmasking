I learned I had autism at 41. Not because anyone caught it. Because I finally went looking, after decades of being told, in performance reviews and friendships and doctors' offices, some version of the same sentence: you're clearly fine.

I want to write about that sentence, because most of the women I know with my wiring got the same one.

## The picture was drawn from boys

The textbook picture of autism came from studying boys. Girls with the same wiring often do something different: we notice we're missing the rules, so we study them. We pick a socially fluent girl and mirror her. We rehearse conversations in the shower. We build, by hand and at enormous cost, a performance of the intuition we don't have.

And it works, which is the trap. A girl who performs well doesn't get flagged for help. She gets called mature, gifted, an old soul. The system reads the mask and closes the file. Decades later she's running a company, and the file is still closed.

## "High functioning" measures your usefulness, not your ease

Be precise about what that label does. High functioning describes how easy you are for everyone else: you hold jobs, you make eye contact you taught yourself, you don't cause problems. It says nothing about the cost on your side of the performance: the scripts, the recovery time after every meeting, the sensory load management nobody sees, the flat exhaustion at the end of a normal day.

So the label becomes a denial mechanism. Ask for accommodation and your own résumé testifies against you. You run a P&L, you clearly don't need help. The reward for coping well is a higher bar and no support. The women at the top of that curve, the executives and founders, get the least support of all, because their evidence of struggle was buried under their evidence of competence.

## What support looks like when nobody built any

I won't pretend there's a program waiting for us. For masked autistic women in their 40s who run companies, formal support is close to nonexistent, and what exists mostly assumes a version of autism we don't present.

What exists is each other. A woman across the table who also taught herself eye contact. Who also needs the quiet corner. Who hears "I found out at 41" and answers with her own number instead of surprise. In those rooms, the whole apparatus of proving and explaining just switches off, and what's left is the conversation you actually needed.

That's not a substitute for the support systems that should exist. But it's real, it's here, and it's monthly: a quiet, seated table in downtown Austin where nobody's competence is held against her.
