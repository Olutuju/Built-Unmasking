Every mask runs on a threat assessment. Yours was written by a child.

She wasn't wrong. In her house, being fully visible had a cost, so she built something that worked: read the weather, perform the safe version, never let the mask slip. It was accurate then.

But that assessment has not been updated in decades. And the system running it doesn't check the date.

## Safety is a fact before it's a feeling

Here's an exercise that sounds too simple and isn't. List the facts. You earn your own money. Your name is on your own lease or deed. You can end any meeting, leave any dinner, block any number. The person whose moods once ran your whole world now has exactly as much access to you as you choose to give.

By every measurable fact, you are safe. The feeling will lag the facts, sometimes by years. That's not a malfunction, it's how deeply the original lesson went in. But you get to start acting on the facts before the feeling catches up. That's what the facts are for.

## Turn it off in one room first

Nobody goes from fully masked to fully visible everywhere, and nobody should. You built the skill for good reasons and some rooms still call for polish. That's fine. The move is smaller: pick one room where the threat level is actually zero, and practice there.

The practice looks unremarkable from outside. Answering "how are you" with the true sentence instead of the automatic one. Letting your face rest. Saying "I'm out of energy tonight" without a cover story. Sitting in a room without scanning it.

What you're collecting is evidence. Every time the true sentence lands and nothing bad happens, the old threat assessment takes an update. Enough updates and the mask becomes what it always should have been: equipment you can put on, instead of skin you can't take off.

## Why this needs other people

You can't collect that evidence alone, because the mask was built for other people. It only comes off around them.

So the room matters. It should be small, quiet, and full of people who also know what a mask weighs, so nobody mistakes your practice for a problem. That is precisely the table I set in downtown Austin: 20 seats, once a month, everyone there fluent in the same thing you're unlearning.
