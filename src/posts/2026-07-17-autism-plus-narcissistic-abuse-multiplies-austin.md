Some math from lived experience: one hard thing plus another hard thing is rarely addition. Sometimes it's multiplication. This is the clearest case of it I know.

## Force one: the house

Narcissistic abuse has one core mechanism, and it isn't cruelty. It's the systematic replacement of your reality with the abuser's. That didn't happen. You're remembering it wrong. You're too sensitive. You made me do that. A child hears this daily, from the people who define what's real, during the exact years she's forming her grip on what's real.

## Force two: the world

Now make that child autistic, in a world not built for her. The lights really are too bright. The noise really is too loud. The social rule really did change without notice. And every time she says so, the world answers with the same sentences the house uses: you're too sensitive, nobody else has a problem, you're taking it wrong.

Here's why that's multiplication and not addition. Each force destroys the evidence you would use to resist the other. When the world dismisses your sensory reality, it confirms the abuser's claim that your perceptions are broken. When the abuser rewrites your history, it teaches you to fold instantly the next time the world says you're wrong. There is no independent ground left to stand on. Every check you'd run against one comes back poisoned by the other.

## What survives that: the mask

A person who cannot trust her own read of any room still has to get through every room. So she stops navigating by her senses and starts navigating by performance. Watch what others do. Mirror it. Script the sentences in advance. Grade yourself constantly. Never, ever improvise.

That's masking at its heaviest, and under those conditions it wasn't a habit or a flaw. It was the only navigation system that still worked. It got her through school, through corporate, often to the top of corporate, because the same system that survives a narcissistic house reads a boardroom effortlessly.

## How reality gets rebuilt

It got torn down through other people, and the people I've sat with say it gets rebuilt the same way: rooms where your account of your own experience is believed the first time you say it. Not argued with, not softened, believed. Enough of those and the ground comes back. For the heaviest parts of this, a therapist is the right professional, and this room doesn't pretend otherwise.
