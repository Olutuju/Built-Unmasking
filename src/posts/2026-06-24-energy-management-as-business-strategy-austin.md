Most productivity advice manages **time** (as if the only scarce resource is the hours in the day. For a chronically ill founder, that's the wrong ledger. You can have a wide-open calendar and no capacity to use it. Your scarce resource isn't time. It's **energy**) and once you treat it like the capital it is, everything about how you run your business in Austin changes.

## Spoon theory is just portfolio theory

If you're in the chronic-illness world you know "[spoon theory](/blog/networking-in-austin-when-your-body-wont-cooperate/)": you start each day with a limited number of spoons, and every task (showering, a meeting, a hard conversation) spends some. Run out and you're done, no overdraft.

Here's what took me too long to see: **that's just portfolio allocation.** You have finite units of a scarce resource. The entire job is to invest them where the return is highest and stop wasting them on low-return holdings. Any finance person reading this already runs this logic on capital every day. [HBR's classic "Manage Your Energy, Not Your Time"](https://hbr.org/) made this case for healthy executives years ago; for us it's not optimization, it's survival.

## Allocate like a strategist, not a martyr

Concretely, that means:

- **Know your peak windows.** Most of us have reliable hours, mornings, or post-rest days. That window is your prime capital. Guard it.
- **Match leverage to energy.** Put your highest-leverage work (the strategy, the pitch, the key decision) into your best window. Never spend peak spoons on email.
- **Cut the low-return drains.** The standing networking event, the meeting that could've been async, the obligation you took on from guilt. Sell those positions. They're eating capital for no return.
- **Keep a reserve.** No serious operator runs at 100% utilization with zero buffer, one shock and you're insolvent. Same with energy. Build in reserve for the flare you can't schedule.

> You are not lazy for protecting your best hours. You're a strategist refusing to squander your scarcest capital on low-return activity. That's just good allocation.

## The calendar is a portfolio statement

Your calendar isn't a to-do list. It's a record of how you invested your scarcest asset. Look at last week's honestly: how much of your peak energy went to genuinely high-return work, and how much bled out to obligations that returned nothing? For most of us the first audit is brutal. It's also where the recovery starts.

This is the same rigor you'd apply to [running a business like a P&L](/blog/corporate-to-founder-executive-rigor-chronic-illness-austin/), just pointed at the one resource that actually constrains you.
