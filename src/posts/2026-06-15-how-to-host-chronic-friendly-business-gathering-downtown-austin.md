Most business events aren't hostile to chronically ill people on purpose. They're hostile by *default*, designed, without thinking, for a body that's reliably energetic, can stand for hours, tolerate noise, and commit weeks ahead. Change a handful of defaults and you get an event where the rest of us can actually show up and thrive. Here's how I host in **downtown Austin**, and why you might too.

## Pick the right venue

- **Central and easy.** Downtown, near transit and with real parking. Every extra mile and every parking hassle is energy spent before anyone's said hello.
- **Quiet and low-stimulation.** A private room, a calm café after hours, a softly lit space. Skip the loud bar. Sensory load is a real, uncounted cost for a lot of your best guests.
- **Genuinely seated.** Enough chairs for everyone to sit the entire time. This one change does more than any other, it silently removes the standing-room tax.

## Design the format for real bodies

- **Keep it small.** A curated dozen beats a crowded hundred. [Depth over breadth](/blog/where-chronically-ill-founders-meet-downtown-austin/) is better networking anyway, and small rooms are far less exhausting.
- **Time-box it.** A clear start and end ("6 to 8") lets people pace themselves. Open-ended "come whenever, stay forever" is quietly stressful.
- **Make come-and-go explicit.** Say it out loud, in the invite: arrive late, leave early, step out, no explanation needed. When you *name* the permission, people actually use it, and a flare costs someone part of an evening instead of the whole thing.
- **Ask about accommodations on RSVP.** "Anything you need to be comfortable, seating, food, sensory?" One line. It tells people this room was built with them in mind.

## The mindset: accessibility is just good hosting

Notice that none of this requires anyone to disclose a thing, or singles anyone out. It's simply *thoughtful hosting* (and here's the part people miss: **it makes the event better for everyone.** The healthy guests also prefer a seat, a quiet room they can actually hear in, and permission to leave when they're done. Designing for the most constrained body in the room lifts the experience for all of them. It's the classic accessibility lesson) the curb cut helps the wheelchair *and* the stroller *and* the traveler with a suitcase, and [Harvard Business Review](https://hbr.org/) has made the same case about inclusive design being better design, full stop.

> You don't need a special "accessible event." You need a well-hosted one. Do it right and nobody has to ask for anything, because you already thought of it.

## This is exactly how our gathering runs

Everything above is the operating manual for the [monthly downtown Austin gathering](/events/), small, seated, quiet, time-boxed, come-and-go, accommodations by default. It's not complicated. It's just built for the people it's for.

If you host anything (a dinner, a salon, a founder meetup) try running it this way. And if you'd rather just come to one that already works, [there's a chair waiting](/community/).
